# Human Evolution

Human evolution is a complex and multifaceted process that has been studied extensively by scientists. According to the Wikipedia article on Human Evolution, the evolution of humans is believed to have occurred between 9 and 7 million years ago, with the genus Homo emerging around 2.8-3.0 million years ago [Source 1].

## Divergence of the Human Clade

The human clade is thought to have diverged from the chimpanzee and bonobo lineages around 9-7 million years ago [Source 1]. This divergence is believed to have occurred in Africa, where the earliest fossils of human-like species, such as Sahelanthropus tchadensis, were found [Source 2].

## Australopithecine and the Emergence of Bipedalism

Around 4-7 million years ago, the Australopithecine species emerged, which includes the extinct biped ancestors of humans [Source 1]. This species is thought to have walked on two legs, with fossil evidence suggesting that they may have had a more human-like pelvis and leg structure than earlier human-like species [Source 3].

## Anatomical Modern Humans

The emergence of anatomically modern humans, Homo sapiens, is believed to have occurred around 300,000 years ago in Africa [Source 1]. This species is characterized by its modern human morphology, including a brain size similar to that of present-day humans and a more modern human-like skull shape [Source 4].

## Evolutionary Process Leading to Anatomically Modern Humans

The evolutionary process leading to anatomically modern humans is believed to have been complex and multifaceted, involving a series of adaptations and innovations that allowed early human species to thrive in different environments [Source 5]. This process was likely driven by a combination of genetic and environmental factors, including climate change, competition for resources, and social and cultural pressures.

## Social and Behavioral Evolution

The social and behavioral evolution of early human species is a topic of ongoing debate among scientists. Some studies have suggested that early human species may have had more juvenile-like characteristics than present-day humans, with a more rapid growth rate and a longer juvenile period [Source 6]. Others have suggested that early human species may have had more chimpanzee-like social and mating behaviors, although this is not supported by fossil evidence [Source 7].
